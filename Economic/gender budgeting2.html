Gender Budgeting in India: Overview

Definition: A policy tool to integrate gender perspectives into fiscal policies, ensuring equitable resource allocation for women’s empowerment.

Historical Background

  • 2005–06: India introduced the Gender Budget Statement (GBS) in the Union Budget.
  • Structure:
    • Part A: Schemes with 100% allocation for women (e.g., Beti Bachao Beti Padhao).
    • Part B: Schemes with ≥30% allocation for women (e.g., PM Awas Yojana).
  • Institutional Framework:
    • Ministry of Women and Child Development (MWCD).
    • Gender Budget Cells in ministries.

Achievements at National Level

  • Increased Allocations:
    • Gender budget rose from 2.8% (2005–06) to 4.3% (2023–24) of total expenditure.
  • Flagship Schemes:
    • Mahila Shakti Kendra: 3.4 lakh women trained (2022–23).
    • Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana: ₹45,000 crore deposits (2023).
  • Health & Education:
    • Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR) fell from 254 (2004–06) to 97 (2018–20).
    • Female literacy rose to 70.3% (2021) from 53.7% (2001).

Critical Challenges in India

  • Inadequate Allocation:
    • Only 5.7% of 2023–24 budget tagged as gender-responsive (NITI Aayog).
  • Tokenism:
    • Siloed Approach: 80% of GBS funds under Part B lack gender-specific outcomes.
  • Implementation Gaps:
    • Funds often diverted (e.g., ₹1,000 crore for BBBP unspent in 2022).
  • Data Deficits:
    • Lack of gender-disaggregated data in sectors like agriculture, urban development.

Gender Budgeting in Bihar: Context

  • Status:
    • Launched Gender Budget Report in 2006–07; allocates ~6% of state budget (2023–24).
  • Key Schemes:
    • Mukhyamantri Kanya Utthan Yojana: ₹54,000 transferred to 10 lakh girls (2023).
    • Jeevika Program: 1.2 crore women in SHGs (2023).
  • Outcomes:
    • MMR: 118 (2020) vs. national average of 97.
    • Female Workforce Participation: 4.1% (2022) vs. national 25.1%.

Critical Challenges in Bihar

  • Patriarchal Norms:
    • Low female literacy (60%) and child marriage rate (40.8%).
  • Weak Institutions:
    • Only 30% panchayats have functional gender budget cells.
  • Fund Misuse:
    • CAG reports (2022) flagged 35% diversion of SC/ST women welfare funds.
  • Intersectional Neglect:
    • Marginalized groups (Muslim women, Dalits) excluded from schemes.

Way Forward

  • Legal Mandate: Enact law to institutionalize gender budgeting (e.g., Kerala Model).
  • Capacity Building: Train officials on gender-responsive planning.
  • Tech Integration: Use AI for real-time monitoring (e.g., DISHA Dashboard).
  • Social Audits: Involve SHGs/NGOs to track outcomes.

Conclusion

While India’s gender budgeting framework is progressive, Bihar exemplifies systemic gaps like patriarchal resistance and weak governance. Addressing these requires decentralized planning, intersectional targeting, and accountability mechanisms. For UPSC, link to SDG 5, federalism, and inclusive growth.
