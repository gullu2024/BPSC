What is Gender Budgeting?

Gender budgeting is a strategy that integrates a gender perspective into the budgetary process to assess how government spending impacts men and women differently [5]. It aims to promote gender equality by ensuring equitable distribution of public resources [5]. The Ministry of Women and Child Development (MoWCD) defines it as a tool to achieve gender mainstreaming, ensuring that development benefits women as much as men [1].

History and Evolution

Gender Budgeting in India was introduced in 2005-06 [2][5]. In 2010, the Planning Commission recommended replacing the Women Component Plan with Gender Responsive Budgeting [4]. By 2021, 27 states and Union Territories had adopted Gender Budgeting [1][4].

Key Components

The Gender Budget Statement (GBS) comprises two parts [2]:

  • Part A: Women Specific Schemes, with 100% allocation for women [2][5].
  • Part B: Pro Women Schemes, where at least 30% of the allocation is for women [2][5].
  • Part C: Schemes with less than 30% provisioning for women [4].

Challenges and Issues

India's Gender Budget remains in the range of 4-6% of the total expenditure and less than 1% of its GDP [1]. A significant portion of gender budgeting is concentrated in five key ministries, with MGNREGA being the biggest scheme [1]. Areas like transportation and water security are often ignored [1].

What Needs to Be Done

To promote gender budgeting, there is a need to move towards gender-sensitive formulation of legislation, programmes and schemes; allocation of resources; implementation and execution; audit and impact assessment of programmes and schemes; and follow-up corrective action to address gender disparities [3].
