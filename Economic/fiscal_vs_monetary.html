Comparative Analysis: Fiscal Policy vs. Monetary Policy

Aspect Fiscal Policy Monetary Policy
Definition Government’s use of taxation and spending Central bank’s control of money supply
Tools Taxes, subsidies, public expenditure Interest rates, CRR, SLR, OMO, repo rate
Authority Government (e.g., Finance Ministry) Central Bank (e.g., RBI, U.S. Fed)
Objective Economic growth, employment, equity Price stability, inflation control
Time Lag Long (Parliament approval needed) Short (RBI can act immediately)
Impact Direct (e.g., MNREGA jobs) Indirect (e.g., borrowing costs)
Political Influence High (e.g., populist schemes) Low (independent central banks)

Critical Analysis

1. Effectiveness in Economic Scenarios

  • Recession:
    • Fiscal Policy: Direct stimulus (e.g., COVID-19 relief packages) boosts demand.
    • Monetary Policy: Lowers interest rates (e.g., RBI’s 2020 repo rate cut to 4%), but limited in liquidity traps.
  • Inflation:
    • Monetary Policy: Effective (e.g., RBI’s 2023 repo rate hike to 6.5% to curb inflation).
    • Fiscal Policy: Indirect (e.g., reducing subsidies to lower fiscal deficit).

2. Coordination Challenges

  • India’s Post-COVID Experience:
    • Fiscal Expansion: ₹20-lakh crore stimulus (5% of GDP) increased debt-to-GDP to 89% (2023).
    • Monetary Support: RBI’s liquidity infusion (₹12 lakh crore) led to inflation spike (7.4% in 2022).
  • Conflict Example: High fiscal deficits force RBI to monetize debt, fueling inflation.

3. Equity vs. Efficiency

  • Fiscal Policy: Redistributive (e.g., PM-KISAN, free ration schemes) but risks crowding out private investment.
  • Monetary Policy: Market-driven (e.g., credit flow) but may widen inequality (asset price boom benefits wealthy).

4. Institutional Constraints

  • Fiscal Policy:
    • FRBM Act: Limits deficit to 3% of GDP (often breached, e.g., 9.5% in 2020–21).
    • Populism: Freebies strain budgets (e.g., Punjab’s ₹18,000 crore power subsidy in 2023).
  • Monetary Policy:
    • Inflation Targeting: RBI’s 4±2% CPI target (successful until COVID).
    • Global Spillovers: Fed rate hikes (2022–23) forced RBI to raise rates despite growth risks.

Case Studies

  1. India’s 1991 Crisis:
    • Fiscal: High deficits (8.4% of GDP) led to BoP crisis.
    • Monetary: Tightened rates to stabilize rupee.
  2. U.S. 2008 Financial Crisis:
    • Fiscal: $700-billion TARP bailout.
    • Monetary: Fed’s near-zero rates and QE.

Way Forward

  1. Synergy: Align fiscal spending with RBI’s inflation goals (e.g., Monetary-Fiscal Coordination Council).
  2. Fiscal Reforms:
    • Rationalize subsidies (e.g., DBT success in LPG).
    • Expand tax base (e.g., GST covers only 1.4 crore firms).
  3. Monetary Innovations:
    • Digital currency (e-CNR) to improve transmission.
    • Green bonds for climate funding.

Conclusion

While fiscal policy is better for equitable growth and crisis response, monetary policy excels in inflation control and agility. However, India’s high public debt and supply-side bottlenecks limit their efficacy. For sustainable growth, coordination (e.g., NK Singh Committee’s debt targets) and structural reforms (e.g., labor laws, infrastructure) are vital.

Flowchart:


Fiscal Policy (Govt.) → Taxes/Spending → Demand Stimulus → Growth/Employment
       ⇅
Monetary Policy (RBI) → Interest Rates → Investment → Inflation Control
