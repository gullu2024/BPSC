Latest Developments in GDP Calculation (Relevant for UPSC)

India’s GDP calculation methodology has undergone significant updates to align with global standards and reflect structural changes in the economy. Below are key developments:

1. Base Year Revision

  • 2011–12 to 2024–25 (Proposed):
    • The Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI) is considering revising the base year to 2024–25 to incorporate new economic activities (e.g., digital economy, gig work).
    • Rationale: Better capture shifts in consumption patterns (e.g., OTT platforms, EVs) and sectoral contributions.
  • Previous Revision: Base year changed from 2004–05 to 2011–12 in 2015.

2. Integration of High-Frequency Data

  • GST Network (GSTN) Data:
    • Real-time tax filings used to track formal sector growth, replacing outdated Annual Survey of Industries (ASI).
  • Corporate Database (MCA21):
    • Expanded coverage of companies (from 5 lakh firms) to improve accuracy of corporate sector estimates.
  • Digital Payments:
    • UPI transactions (~₹18.3 lakh crore in March 2024) used to gauge informal sector activity.

3. New Methodology for Informal Sector

  • Unorganised Sector Estimates:
    • Use of Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS) and e-Shram Portal (28 crore informal workers registered) to refine informal sector contributions.
  • Challenges: Underreporting persists due to cash-based transactions.

4. Green GDP Initiatives

  • Pilot Project on Natural Capital Accounting:
    • MoSPI launched a trial to measure environmental costs (e.g., deforestation, pollution) deducted from GDP.
    • Aligns with UN System of Environmental-Economic Accounting (SEEA).
  • Significance: Addresses sustainability in line with SDGs and COP26 commitments.

5. Sector-Specific Updates

  • IT/ITES and Startups:
    • Revised weights for IT sector (9.3% of GDP in 2023) and inclusion of unicorns (e.g., Flipkart, Zomato).
  • Agriculture:
    • Use of remote sensing and AI for crop yield estimation (e.g., FASAL scheme).

6. Global Alignment

  • UN System of National Accounts (SNA 2008):
    • India adopted SNA 2008 in 2015, replacing SNA 1993.
    • Changes include:
      • Valuing crops at harvest prices (not farm gate).
      • Including R&D expenditure as capital formation.

Critical Analysis for UPSC

1. Advantages

  • Accuracy: High-frequency data (GSTN, MCA21) reduces lags and improves reliability.
  • Policy Relevance: Reflects structural shifts (e.g., gig economy, digital India).

2. Challenges

  • Data Gaps: Informal sector (45% of GDP) remains undercounted.
  • Controversies: Post-2011–12 revision faced criticism for overestimating growth (e.g., 2016–17 growth revised from 7.1% to 8.3%).
  • Green GDP Hurdles: Lack of standardized metrics for environmental costs.

3. Case Study

  • COVID-19 Impact: GDP contraction (-6.6% in FY21) highlighted reliance on proxy data during crises.

UPSC-Focused Discussion Points

  • GS Paper III: Link GDP revisions to economic planning, employment, and sustainability.
  • Ethics/Governance: Discuss transparency in data (e.g., delays in PLFS reports).
  • Global Examples: Compare with China’s GDP recalibration (services sector boost) or EU’s green accounting.

Conclusion

India’s evolving GDP methodology aims to balance global standards with domestic realities. While innovations like GSTN integration and green accounting are commendable, challenges like informal sector measurement and political misuse of data persist. For UPSC, focus on interlinking GDP reforms with policy outcomes (e.g., Atmanirbhar Bharat, SDGs) and emphasize critical thinking on data credibility.
