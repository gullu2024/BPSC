Gender Budgeting in India

Introduction

Gender budgeting is a fiscal strategy that integrates gender perspectives into budgetary decisions to ensure equitable resource allocation for men and women. In India, it was formally introduced in 2005-06 to address gender disparities through policy interventions.

Objectives of Gender Budgeting

  • Ensure gender equality in resource distribution
  • Address socio-economic disparities in education, health, and employment
  • Promote women’s participation in decision-making and workforce
  • Make policies gender-responsive and inclusive

Evolution of Gender Budgeting in India

  1. Pre-2005 Era: Policies focused on women's welfare but lacked structured gender budgeting.
  2. 2005-06 Onward: The Ministry of Finance and Ministry of Women & Child Development institutionalized gender budgeting.
  3. Current Status: Gender budgeting is implemented across multiple ministries and states, with specific allocations for women-centric programs.

Mechanism of Gender Budgeting

  • Gender Budgeting Cells (GBCs): Established in ministries/departments.
  • Categorization of Expenditure:
    • Part A: 100% allocation for women (e.g., Maternity Benefit Scheme)
    • Part B: At least 30% allocation benefits women
  • Annual Gender Budget Statement: Included in Union Budget since 2005-06.

Key Government Initiatives under Gender Budgeting

  • Beti Bachao Beti Padhao – Focus on education and welfare of the girl child.
  • Mahila E-Haat – Online marketing platform for women entrepreneurs.
  • Pradhan Mantri Matru Vandana Yojana (PMMVY) – Financial aid for pregnant women.
  • National Creche Scheme – Childcare support for working mothers.
  • Ujjwala Scheme – Free LPG connections to improve women’s health.

Impact of Gender Budgeting in India

  • ✅ Improved female literacy rates and reduced dropout rates.
  • ✅ Increased female workforce participation in various sectors.
  • ✅ Better maternal and child health indicators due to targeted health programs.
  • ✅ Enhanced women’s access to financial services and entrepreneurship opportunities.

Challenges in Gender Budgeting

  • ❌ Lack of gender-disaggregated data for effective policy planning.
  • ❌ Insufficient awareness and training among policymakers.
  • ❌ Budgeting gaps and fund underutilization in several ministries.
  • ❌ State-level implementation disparities due to governance issues.

Way Forward

  • Strengthening Gender Budgeting Cells (GBCs) for better execution.
  • Encouraging private sector participation in gender-sensitive policies.
  • Enhancing data collection and monitoring of gender-based expenditures.
  • Increasing fund allocation to critical sectors like education, health, and employment.

Conclusion

Gender budgeting in India is a crucial tool for women’s empowerment and economic inclusion. While progress has been made, stronger policy enforcement, better fund utilization, and deeper integration of gender-sensitive policies are essential for achieving true gender equality.
